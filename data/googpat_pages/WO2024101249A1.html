Viral infection inhibitors
The present invention relates to a virus infection inhibitor.

Viruses are protein structures that contain nucleic acids to utilize host cells to self-transform. Due to this characteristic, there has been a long-standing debate as to whether they are living or non-living.
If viruses are considered as living organisms, they are the most numerous on Earth, with an estimated 10 31 viruses in existence (Non-Patent Document 1). Historically, viruses have played a wide range of roles in ecosystems, and they have played important roles in the evolution of animals, with a clear trace being the incorporation of genes essential for placental formation, which is important during pregnancy, into the human genome (an example of exsuperation, Non-Patent Document 2). Macroscopically, viruses have been reported to affect the balance of ecosystems and to be involved in the suppression of red tides in the ocean (Non-Patent Document 3).
Viruses thus have a wide-ranging effect on the physiological functions and ecosystems of animals and plants, but their generally known function is as a pathogen. The spread of infection by a virulent pathogenic virus can cause enormous damage and become a serious social problem. Examples of such pathogenic viruses include coronaviruses, influenza viruses, and swine fever viruses. All of these are enveloped RNA viruses, and are known to cause infectious diseases in humans and animals.

Various types of coronaviruses are known, including the porcine epidemic diarrhea (PED) virus, which belongs to the alphacoronavirus and causes acute diarrhea in pigs; the SARS coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), which belongs to the betacoronavirus and caused the recent global pandemic of novel coronavirus disease (COVID-19); the avian infectious bronchitis virus (IB), which belongs to the gammacoronavirus; and seasonal influenza viruses. In addition, avian influenza (H5N1) virus and swine fever virus (family Flaviviridae, genus Pestivirus) are of concern not only for their danger but also for the serious economic harm that may result from culling.
Due to the nature of RNA viruses, these viruses undergo rapid genetic mutations and can produce various subspecies in a relatively short period of time, meaning that unknown mutant strains are constantly emerging. Inevitably, different mutant strains may exist in different regions, creating the problem that vaccines effective against mutant strains cannot be developed in time.
Due to these issues, there is an urgent need to develop technology to inhibit viral infection, and various technologies have been proposed to prevent and inhibit coronavirus infection (e.g., Patent Documents 1 and 2).

However, definitive infection control technologies mainly use artificially synthesized pharmaceutical ingredients such as ethanol and benzalkonium chloride, and do not take into account the conservation of ecosystems or interactions with symbiotic bacteria that live in symbiosis with humans and animals.
Therefore, even if it has an infection suppressing effect against viruses, it is expected that it may cause other harmful effects. That is, as proposed in the "hygiene hypothesis" proposed in 1989, in modern society, excessive hygiene management is a trigger for the outbreak of different diseases (Non-Patent Document 4). Therefore, it is necessary to realize the infection suppression of pathogenic viruses in a form that maintains a healthy form of symbiosis between humans and animals and microorganisms.
JP 2000-044473 A JP 2022-067656 A

There is a demand for technology that does not have the adverse effects on ecosystems or symbiotic microorganisms of humans and animals, as artificially synthesized drugs do, and that is also effective in suppressing the infectivity of viruses in general, which have high mutation rates and require time to develop vaccines.
It is expected that this technology will not only cut off the source of infection, but also enable operations for a sustainable society from the perspective of further infection prevention measures.
The present invention was made in consideration of the above situation, and aims to provide a new environmentally friendly infection inhibitor for infectious diseases viruses (RNA viruses and DNA viruses).
The inventors discovered that the above problems could be solved by using a specific mixture of microorganisms, and thus completed the present invention. Specifically, the present invention provides the following:
(1) A viral infection inhibitor containing a microorganism of the genus Caldibacillus or a culture thereof.
(2) The virus infection inhibitor according to (1), wherein the Caldibacillus genus microorganism includes NITE accession number "BP-863."

(3) An environmentally symbiotic virus infection inhibitor comprising a mixed microorganism having a symbiotic function with humans, animals, plants, and ecosystems,
The viral infection inhibitor according to (1), wherein the mixed microorganisms are those of the genus Caldibacillus and Paenibacillus.
(4) An environmentally symbiotic virus infection inhibitor according to (3), comprising the mixed microorganism having NITE accession number "BP-03693."
(5) The environmentally symbiotic viral infection inhibitor according to (4), wherein the mixed microorganisms including NITE accession number "BP-03693" include ATCC accession number "PTA-1773".
(6) The virus infection inhibitor according to (5), wherein the ATCC accession number "PTA-1773" includes the NITE accession number "BP-863" and the NITE accession number "BP-1051".
(7) The viral infection inhibitor according to any one of (1) to (6), wherein the virus is porcine epidemic diarrhea virus belonging to alphacoronavirus, SARS-CoV-2 belonging to betacoronavirus, or avian infectious bronchitis virus (IB) belonging to gammacoronavirus.
(8) The viral infection inhibitor according to any one of (1) to (6), wherein the virus is a seasonal influenza virus, an avian influenza (H5N1 type) virus, or a swine fever virus (family Flaviviridae, genus Pestivirus).
(9) A virus infection inhibitor that does not adversely affect symbiotic organisms, according to any one of (1) to (6), in which the virus is a Caliciviridae virus, such as norovirus, feline calicivirus, norovirus, Norwalk virus, rabbit hemorrhagic disease virus, plant-infecting viruses (begomovirus, tobamovirus, nepovirus, tobravirus), bacteriophage (cystovirus, Cystoviridae), or hepatitis virus.
The present invention provides a novel infection inhibitor for environmental symbiotic viruses (RNA viruses and DNA viruses).

The following describes in detail an embodiment of the present invention. Note that the present invention is not limited to the following embodiment.

<Viral infection inhibitor>
The viral infection inhibitor of the present invention (hereinafter also referred to as "the infection inhibitor of the present invention") comprises a microorganism of the genus Caldibacillus or a culture thereof.

The infection inhibitor of the present invention may preferably contain one or more bacteria selected from the group consisting of ATCC Accession No. "PTA-1773", NITE Accession No. "BP-03693", and NITE Accession No. "BP-863".
All of the above include microorganisms of the genus Caldibacillus.

In the present invention, the term "microbial culture" includes any culture obtained by culturing a microorganism under conditions (medium, temperature, etc.) that allow the microorganism to grow. The culture liquid may be, for example, a culture liquid containing a microorganism (sterile bacterial liquid, non-sterile bacterial liquid, etc.), a culture supernatant not containing a microorganism, etc.
The medium may include animal or vegetable proteins.


(1) ATCC deposit number "PTA-1773"
The mixed microorganism "PTA-1773" is a thermophilic inoculum deposited on May 10, 2000 with the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC), an international depository authority under the Budapest Treaty on the International Recognition of the Deposit of Microorganisms for the Purposes of Patent Procedure, 10801 University Boulevard Manassas, Virginia 20110-2209 U.S.A.
ATCC accession number "PTA-1773" includes NITE accession number "BP-863", NITE accession number "BP-1051", and NITE accession number "BP-03693".
"PTA-1773" contains microorganisms of the genera Caldibacillus and Paenibacillus.



(2) NITE accession number: BP-03693
The mixed microorganism "BP-03693" is a thermophilic seed bacterium deposited on July 27, 2022 by the National Institute of Technology and Evolution (address: 2-5-8 Kazusa Kamatari, Kisarazu City, Chiba Prefecture), an international depository authority under the Budapest Treaty on the International Recognition of the Deposit of Microorganisms for Patent Procedure.
The mixed microorganism "BP-03693" was isolated from the mixed microorganism "PTA-1773" and consists of three strains: Caldibacillus hisashii (NITE accession number "BP-863", international accession dated February 8, 2010), Paenibacillus No. 39 strain, and No. 139 strain.
In addition, the National Institute of Technology and Evolution (address: 2-5-8 Kazusa Kamatari, Kisarazu City, Chiba Prefecture) confirmed on October 31, 2022 that "BP-03693" is composed of three strains: Caldibacillus hisashii, Paenibacillus No. 39, and No. 139.



At the time of deposit, "BP-863" was internationally deposited as a closely related species of Bacillus thermoamylovans (January 15, 2010), but was subsequently registered as a new species of Bacillus hisashii (Nishida, A. et al. Bacillus hisashii sp. nov., isolated from the caeca of gnotobiotic mice fed with thermophile-fermented compost. Int J Syst Evol Microbiol 65, 3944-3949, doi:10.1099/ijsem.0.000516(2015)).
Furthermore, due to changes in the international classification of microorganisms, the name was changed to Caldibacillus hisashii (Gupta, R. S., Patel, S., Saini, N. & Chen, S. Robust demarcation of 17 distinct Bacillus species clades, proposed as novel Bacillaceae genera, by phylogenomics and comparative genomic analyses: description of Robertmuraya kyonggiensis sp. nov. and Proposal for an emended genus Bacillus limiting it only to the members of the Subtilis and Cereus clades of species. Int J Syst Evol Microbiol 70, 5753-5798, doi: 10.1099/ijsem. 0.004475 (2020)).
As described in Gupta et al., with the above-mentioned classification change, it was determined that the conventional bacteria Bacillus thermoamylovorans and Bacillus hisashii are closely related bacteria, and both are classified in the genus Caldibacillus.
In addition, the National Institute of Technology and Evolution (address: 2-5-8 Kazusa Kamatari, Kisarazu City, Chiba Prefecture) certified that "BP-863" is classified as Caldibacillus hisashii on October 31, 2022.
"BP-03693" includes microorganisms of the genera Caldibacillus and Paenibacillus.

(3) NITE accession number: BP-1051
The mixed microorganism "BP-1051" is a thermophilic seed bacterium deposited on January 18, 2011 by the National Institute of Technology and Evolution (address: 2-5-8 Kazusa Kamatari, Kisarazu City, Chiba Prefecture), an international depository authority under the Budapest Treaty on the International Recognition of the Deposit of Microorganisms for the Purposes of Patent Procedure.


(4) Effects of the Infection Inhibitor of the Present Invention, etc. In a previous study, the present inventors found that "PTA-1773" and "BP-863" can activate the innate immune system of animals, and furthermore, that they reduce fat accumulation by controlling the intestinal bacterial flora (WO2011/099514). In addition, "PTA-1773" and "BP-1051" are effective for plant growth and have the function of suppressing the generation of greenhouse gases derived from soil.
As a result of further investigation, it was found that a fermented solution of a mixed microorganism containing "BP-03693" contained in "PTA-1773" has a significant infection-suppressing effect against at least certain viruses (e.g., RNA viruses (coronaviruses, influenza viruses, etc.) and DNA viruses (hepatitis B virus, etc.)).
In the present invention, "mixed microorganisms" refers to a congregation of multiple types of microorganisms. As a result of genome analysis of the deposited microorganisms, the possible presence of phage-related proteins and lantibiotic peptides has been confirmed. Therefore, these may be involved in suppressing viral infection.

In the present invention, the "viral infection suppressing effect" includes the suppression of viral proliferation in the presence of the infection suppressor of the present invention.
The effect of suppressing viral infection can be evaluated by the method shown in the Examples.
Furthermore, the above deposited microorganisms (particularly those including microorganisms of the genus Caldibacillus and Paenibacillus) are expected to have symbiotic functions with humans, animals, plants, and ecosystems, and to be environmentally symbiotic.
In the present invention, "environmentally symbiotic" includes not damaging the balance of bacterial flora, such as the normal flora of the skin and the normal flora of the intestines. For example, existing disinfectants (such as ethanol) tend to damage the balance of such bacterial flora, and are therefore not considered to be environmentally symbiotic.
The infection inhibitor of the present invention is expected to be environmentally symbiotic, and therefore in addition to suppressing viral infectivity, it can also reduce adverse effects on ecosystems and various environments (e.g., soil, wastewater, inside the body, rivers, seawater, bottom marshes, etc.) (Figure 4).
The infection inhibitor of the present invention is expected to be environmentally symbiotic, which may enable skin disinfection, nasal disinfection, etc., with minimal burden, as well as disinfection of livestock sheds, etc. (Figure 5).

(5) Constitution of the Infection Inhibitor of the Present Invention, etc. The infection inhibitor of the present invention is not particularly limited, except that it may contain a microorganism of the genus Caldibacillus (e.g., "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", or "BP-863", etc.) or a culture thereof. It should be noted that "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" are included in "PTA-1773".

The content of each of "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051" and "BP-863" can be appropriately set depending on the infection suppression effect to be obtained, etc.
The content (dry weight) of each of "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" can be, for example, 0.001% by mass or more and 100% by mass or less relative to the infection inhibitor of the present invention.
The infection inhibitor of the present invention may contain any additives (microorganisms other than "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863", solvents (water, etc.), excipients, buffers, preservatives, etc.) to the extent that they do not inhibit the action of "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863". The types and amounts of such additives can be appropriately set depending on the effect to be obtained, etc.
In a preferred embodiment of the infection inhibitor of the present invention, the constituent microorganisms consist only of "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863".

The infection suppressant of the present invention may be in the form of a liquid, a solid, or the like.
The infection suppressant of the present invention may be in a form containing microbial cells (such as a bacterial cell culture solution) or may be a microbial culture supernatant (not containing bacterial cells), for example.
The infection inhibitor of the present invention can be produced by conventionally known methods depending on its form.
Since "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" are thermophilic, they may be sterilized before being added to the infection inhibitor of the present invention. By performing a sterilization process, "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" can be added in a state where they are prevented from being contaminated by various bacteria.
The sterilization temperature for "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" can be 40 to 100C.
The sterilization time for "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", and "BP-863" can be 1 to 120 minutes.

(6) Viruses The type of virus whose infection is inhibited by the infection inhibitor of the present invention is not particularly limited, and may be any coronavirus that infects mammals (humans, pets (dogs, cats, etc.), livestock (pigs, etc.), etc.), and its mutant strains.
Viruses include both RNA and DNA viruses.
RNA viruses include porcine epidemic diarrhea virus, which belongs to the alphacoronavirus, SARS-CoV-2, which belongs to the betacoronavirus, and infectious bronchitis virus (IB), which belongs to the gammacoronavirus.
Examples of RNA viruses include seasonal influenza viruses, avian influenza (H5N1) viruses, and swine fever viruses (family Flaviviridae, genus Pestivirus).
RNA viruses include plant viruses, bacteriophages, etc., as well as non-enveloped RNA viruses such as feline calicivirus, which is a member of the Caliciviridae family and causes respiratory disease in cats, norovirus, which causes infectious gastroenteritis in humans, Norwalk virus, and rabbit hemorrhagic disease virus, which causes hemorrhagic disease in rabbits.
Coronaviruses include those belonging to the Orthocoronavirus subfamily. These coronaviruses are divided into four genera (Alphacoronavirus, Betacoronavirus, Gammacoronavirus, and Deltacoronavirus).
Coronaviruses of the alphacoronavirus genus include porcine epidemic diarrhea virus and human coronavirus 229E.
Coronaviruses in the Betacoronavirus genus include SARS-associated coronaviruses (SARS coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) etc.), Betacoronavirus 1, and mouse coronavirus.
Examples of coronaviruses in the gammacoronavirus genus include infectious bronchitis virus.
Examples of coronaviruses in the Deltacoronavirus genus include brown-eared bulbul coronavirus HKU11.
Influenza viruses include swine influenza (H1N1) virus, seasonal influenza, and avian influenza (H5N1) virus.
Examples of swine fever viruses include swine fever viruses of the genus Pestivirus in the family Flaviviridae.
An example of a DNA virus is the hepatitis B virus (HBV).

From the viewpoint of easily obtaining the effects of the present invention, the targets of the infection inhibitor of the present invention are preferably porcine epidemic diarrhea virus, SARS coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), swine influenza virus, hepatitis B virus (HBV), and the like.
Other preferred viruses include the Caliciviridae family of noroviruses, feline caliciviruses, noroviruses, Norwalk viruses, rabbit hemorrhagic disease viruses, plant-infecting viruses (begomoviruses, tobamoviruses, nepoviruses, tobraviruses), bacteriophages (cystoviruses, Cystoviridae), or hepatitis viruses.

(4) Method for inhibiting viral infection using the infection inhibitor of the present invention Viral infection can be inhibited by contacting the infection inhibitor of the present invention with a virus by any method.
The method of contacting the infection inhibitor of the present invention with the virus is not particularly limited, but examples include spraying the infection inhibitor of the present invention into the air of a subject suspected of having the virus, applying it as a nasal drop, or wiping the subject with an impregnated material (such as tissue paper soaked in the infection inhibitor of the present invention).
Subjects that the infection inhibitor of the present invention may come into contact with include not only living organisms (e.g., the surface of the skin, inside the nostrils, etc.), but also various articles that may come into contact with living organisms, such as household goods (e.g., the surfaces of furniture, electrical appliances, miscellaneous goods, etc.) and various types of equipment (e.g., the surfaces of animal care equipment such as fences and cages).



An example of a social implementation of the present invention is illustrated in FIGS.
As shown in FIG. 6, the infection inhibitor of the present invention can be placed in a container such as a tank and permanently installed in various facilities.
In this embodiment, as shown in Fig. 7, when an exotic animal enters the facility, it is detected by an infrared sensor or the like, and the infection inhibitor of the present invention can be sprayed onto the exotic animal. As a result, it becomes difficult for infectious sources such as viruses to enter the facility.
In addition, the infection inhibitor of the present invention is less likely to have a negative effect on intestinal bacteria, unlike ordinary disinfectants, etc. In other words, the present invention leads to the conservation of environmental symbiotic systems, and therefore is in line with the global perspective of biodiversity protection (nature positive).

<Test 1: Preparation of virus (RNA virus) infection inhibitor>
Samples were prepared using mixed microorganisms in the following manner.

The mixed microorganisms having ATCC accession number "PTA-1773" were diluted 100-fold with water and heated at 50C for at least 1 hour.
After heating, impurities were removed by filtration through a filter with a pore size of 100 m, and the obtained filtrate was used as a sample (corresponding to the infection inhibitor of the present invention) for the following tests.

<Test 2: Test for suppression of infection with viruses (RNA viruses)-1>
The sample obtained in "Test 1" was used to examine the effect of inhibiting infection with an RNA virus. The following test was conducted by Shokukan Sanitation Research Institute Co., Ltd.


(1) Preparation of Virus and Cultured Cells Porcine epidemic diarrhea virus (PEDV, P-5V strain), a swine-infectious coronavirus, was prepared.
Vero cells (a cell line derived from the kidney epithelium of African green monkeys) were used as cultured cells.

(2) Inoculation of the virus The sample was contacted with the virus according to the following method. The following test was performed with reference to "Virus Experiments: General Theory, Revised 2nd Edition, Maruzen Co., Ltd., Virus Neutralization Test Method."



(2-1) Preliminary Test Prior to the main test, the effect of the sample on cultured cells (cytotoxicity) was confirmed.
First, the sample was serially diluted 10-fold with phosphate buffer and then inoculated onto cultured cells. The highest concentration showing a normal state of the cells after culture was confirmed, and the virus concentration to be used in the test was determined.
As a result, no cytotoxicity was observed in the 10-fold diluted solution, and therefore the detection limit in this test was set at 10 1.5 TCID 50 /mL.


(2-2) Main Test According to the test classification shown in Table 1, the sample and phosphate buffer solution (10 mL each) were each taken, and the virus solution (1 mL) was added at the concentration determined in the preliminary test.
After the addition of the virus solution, the obtained test solution was allowed to stand at room temperature (25 C.) for 3 hours.


Next, each test solution after the reaction was completed was serially diluted 10-fold, and 100 L of each solution was inoculated onto cultured cells in a 96-well plate.
After inoculation, the mixture was cultured at 37 C. in carbon dioxide gas (5%) for 5 days.
The obtained cultured cells were observed under a microscope, and the presence or absence of viral proliferation was confirmed based on CPE (cytopathic effect) appearing in the cultured cells, and the viral concentration was calculated for each test category.

Based on the obtained values, the virus reduction rate (%) of the test group relative to the control group was calculated using the following formula.
Virus reduction rate (%) = (control group - test group) / control group x 100



Based on the above results, the reduction rate (%) of the test group relative to the control group was calculated 3 hours after the start of sensitization, and the infection suppression effect was confirmed.
The reduction rate was calculated using the following formula.
Log reduction value=Log 10 (virus titer in control group)Log 10 (virus titer in test group)
Reduction rate (%) = (1-1/10 log reduction value ) x 100





(3) Results The results are shown in FIG. 1 and Table 2.
In the control group, a natural attenuation of the viral load was observed from the start of the test (time of virus addition) until 3 hours after the start of the test (10 6.1 10 5.7 TCID 50 /mL).
In contrast, in the test group, a significant decrease in the viral load was observed 3 hours after the start of the test (<10 1.5 TCID 50 /mL, reduction rate=99.99% or more).
Therefore, it was found that the thermophilic bacteria fermentation liquid, which was the sample obtained in "Test 1", had an inactivation effect of 99.99% against the porcine epidemic diarrhea virus after a reaction time of 180 minutes.
From the above results, it was found that the infection inhibitor of the present invention has an excellent infection-suppressing effect against porcine epidemic diarrhea virus.

In contrast, in the test group, a significant decrease in the viral load was observed 3 hours after the start of the test (<10 1.9 TCID 50 /mL or 10 1.7 TCID 50 /mL, reduction rate = 99.99% in both cases).
From the above results, it was found that the infection inhibitor of the present invention has excellent infection suppression effect against SARS coronavirus 2.

<Test 6: Test for suppression of infection with viruses (RNA viruses)-5>
The sample obtained in "Test 1" was used to examine the inhibitory effect on infection with avian infectious bronchitis virus. The following test was carried out by Shokukan Sanitation Research Institute Co., Ltd.

(1) Preparation of Virus and Cultured Cells An avian infectious virus, infectious bronchitis virus (IBV), H120 strain (Poulvac IB H120, Kyoritsu Seiyaku Co., Ltd.), was prepared.

(2) Inoculation of the virus The sample was contacted with the virus according to the following method. The following test was performed with reference to "Virus Experiments: General Theory, Revised 2nd Edition, Maruzen Co., Ltd., Virus Neutralization Test Method."



(2-1) Preparation of test solution A virus solution was prepared by dissolving the live vaccine in purified water to give a concentration of 10 5.0 EID 50 or more per mL.
Next, according to the test classification shown in Table 6, the sample and phosphate buffer solution (10 mL each) were each taken, and the virus solution (1 mL) was added thereto.
After the addition of the virus solution, the obtained test solution was allowed to stand at room temperature (25 C.) for a period of time determined for each test category.




(2-2) Inoculation into Embryonated Chicken Eggs After completion of sensitization, the test solution in each test group was filtered through a membrane filter (0.45 m) and serially diluted 10-fold.
Each dilution was then inoculated into the allantoic cavity of 10-day-old embryonated eggs from a flock of chickens (Lohmann VALO) at 0.1 mL each, with five embryonated eggs for each dilution step, and three controls inoculated with phosphate buffer only (0.1 mL).
After inoculation, the eggs were cultured for 7 days at 37 C. Seven days after inoculation, the eggs were opened, and the condition of the embryos in each test group was confirmed using the control chicken embryos as a standard.
Chicken embryos that died or changed (stunted growth, dwarfism) were evaluated as "infected," and the logarithm of the minimum dilution rate of the test solution at which an infection-suppressing effect was observed was calculated as the virus titer (Behrens-Karber method).



Based on the above results, the reduction rate (%) of the test group relative to the control group was calculated 3 hours after the start of sensitization, and the infection suppression effect was confirmed.
The reduction rate was calculated using the following formula.
Log reduction value=Log 10 (virus titer in control group)Log 10 (virus titer in test group)
Reduction rate (%) = (1-1/10 log reduction value ) x 100

In the control group, natural attenuation was observed within 3 hours after the start of sensitization, and the virus titer changed from 10 5.9 EID 50 /mL to approximately 10 5.3 EID 50 /mL.

<Test 7: Test for suppression of infection with viruses (DNA viruses)>
Preliminary tests were carried out using Hepatitis B virus (HBV) as a DNA virus.
As a result, in the presence of "PTA-1773,""BP-03693,""BP-1051," or "BP-863," or a sterilized solution thereof, an infectivity-suppressing effect was confirmed, similar to that of the above-mentioned RNA viruses.
Regarding HBV, it was confirmed that within 30 minutes of exposure to "PTA-1773,""BP-03693,""BP-1051," or "BP-863," or a sterile solution thereof (sterile filtered with a 0.22 micrometer filter), the amount of HBV present tended to decrease by about 103 cells.
The above results suggest that the agent of the present invention may be able to control not only RNA viruses but also DNA viruses through the action of extracellular metabolites derived from the bacterial cells.




In addition, a plant-infecting DNA virus, Tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV), is known to be transmitted by whiteflies.
It has been confirmed in agricultural fields that when a fermentation product containing "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", or "BP-863" is applied, there is a tendency for yellow leaf curl disease to not occur, even though there is not necessarily an adverse effect on whiteflies.
In addition, root-knot nematodes transmit plant-infectious RNA viruses such as nepovirus and tobravirus. On the other hand, it has been found that when a fermentation product containing "PTA-1773", "BP-03693", "BP-1051", or "BP-863" is applied to soil in which root-knot nematodes are abundant, crop damage is unlikely to occur, even though the number of root-knot nematodes present does not decrease.
The phenomenon observed in these plants suggests that the agent of the present invention may have an inhibitory effect on viral infectivity against a wide range of plant-infecting DNA viruses and RNA viruses.
These results suggest that the compound may have a direct virus infectivity-inhibiting effect on, for example, Begomovirus of the Geminiviridae family, which is the plant-infectious DNA virus TYLCV, Tobamovirus of the Virgaviridae family, known as Tomato mosaic virus, and the plant-infectious RNA viruses nepovirus and tobravirus, without having any adverse effects on the symbiont organisms themselves.



<Industrial Applicability of the Invention>
The hygiene hypothesis (Strachan DP. Hayfever, hygiene, and household size. BMJ 299:1259-1260, 1989) described in the Background Art section points out concerns about excessive hygiene management.
Specifically, it has been suggested that the degree of hygiene during infancy affects the future incidence of allergies, and that an unhygienic environment may reduce the later onset of allergies.
Since then, this hypothesis has been widely accepted, and a large amount of data supporting it has been accumulated, and the relationship between the formation of the intestinal microbiota and other diseases has also been pointed out. In other words, it suggests that creating an environment in which animals can coexist with environmental microbial communities is important for their survival.

On the other hand, the form and degree of this symbiosis are the subject of debate, and it is difficult to set strict standards. The base technology of the present application is known to have a wide range of positive effects on animals and plants, such as activating the immune system, reducing fat accumulation, promoting plant growth, and suppressing the generation of greenhouse gases that are a factor in environmental load, among others.
On this basis, it will be possible to suppress viral infection, which is a completely different perspective from conventional viral infection inhibitors.


For example, the bacteriophage Cystoviridae, which is also an RNA virus, can infect actinomycetes, a symbiotic soil microorganism involved in plant growth. Therefore, solutions containing "BP-863" or "BP-03693" may suppress the death of actinomycetes and contribute to plant growth.
In addition, the bacteriophage "Cystoviridae," which is also an RNA virus, can infect "Lactococcus," which is also known as a probiotic for fish and the like.
Therefore, a solution containing "BP-863" or "BP-03693" may suppress the infectivity of the bacteriophage "Cystoviridae", which is also an RNA virus, and suppress the death of "Lactococcus", thereby increasing the production of functional molecules derived from "Lactococcus". In fact, when the intestinal flora of fish was evaluated, it was confirmed that "Lactococcus" tended to increase in saltwater fish and freshwater fish fed fermented feed containing "BP-863" or "BP-03693", which is a consistent trend.


It has also been pointed out that insects such as nematodes are vectors for the transmission of plant pathogenic viruses, etc. When these viruses are RNA viruses, the product of the present invention can reduce the adverse effects on plants.
It is expected that the mechanism of action may involve 2-aminoisobutyric acid (-aminoisobutyric acid, hereinafter referred to as Aib) and its complexes.
Aib is often called an abnormal amino acid, and unlike normal amino acids, it is not a normal protein component. Due to its characteristics, it is known to be one of the components that play an antibiotic role, such as a lantibiotic. Research results in synthetic chemistry suggest that Aib may form a complex with papain (Tsuchiya, K. & Numata, K. Chemoenzymatic synthesis of polypeptides containing the unnatural amino acid 2-aminoisobutyric acid. Chem Commun (Camb) 53, 7318-7321, doi:10.1039/c7cc03095a (2017)) and may be transformed into a complex with high cell permeability (Terada, K. et al. Artificial Cell-Penetrating Peptide Containing Periodic alpha-Aminoisobutyric Acid with Long-Term Internalization Efficiency in Human and Plant Cells. ACS Biomater Sci Eng 6, 3287-3298, doi: 10.1021/acsbiomaterials. 0c00182 (2020)).



On the other hand, "BP-863" contained in "BP-03693", i.e., Caldibacillus hisashii, and Paenibacillus No. 36 strain contained in "BP-03693", have putative papain-like cysteine peptidase (SEQ ID NO: 1) and utative papain-like cysteine peptidase (DUF1796) (SEQ ID NO: 2) as enzymes similar to papain. It is expected that these enzymes may be involved in the synthesis of lantibiotics with Aib as a constituent factor.
Furthermore, after extracting the genomic DNA of these strains, sequence information was identified as a result of whole genome analysis (Illumina HiSeq).
Furthermore, a gene (SEQ ID NO: 3) closely related to the lantibiotic-related gene (sunA; lantibiotic antibiotic) possessed by Bacillus thermoamylovorans was also detected in the genome genes of "BP-863".
It is hoped that these factors work together to be effective in suppressing viral infection.
In addition, it is expected that the environmental symbiotic function will be improved by suppressing the types and populations of viruses in animals where symbiotic bacteria exist in the nose, oral cavity, skin, bronchi, intestines, etc., and in natural environments where symbiotic bacteria can exist in soil, wastewater, rivers, seawater, bottom mud, etc.

In building a sustainable society in the future, it will be extremely important to consider the symbiosis of the entire ecosystem, and it will not be enough to simply deal with pathogens.
This application is expected to be an important technology in these respects.

One method of using the present invention would be to slightly dilute the concentration of the viral infection inhibitor for humans and then add this technology while minimizing the inhibition of symbiotic microorganisms.
In addition, because of its low environmental impact, it may be used in a variety of situations, such as in soil, wastewater, and feed to suppress viruses, or by applying it to the nose or skin, or by spraying it in rooms and livestock sheds, or in combination with air purifiers, where it is necessary to suppress viral infection but not to reduce beneficial symbiotic bacteria.
