The invention relates to a nucleic acid encoding a new human endothelial cell adhesion protein, designated VAP-1, which has an adhesive function and aminoxidase activity. The invention also relates to the use of a nucleic acid and a VAP-1 polypeptide.


. , . , , . , , , . , , , . , , , , .BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION
The continuous circulation of lymphocytes between blood and tissue is crucial for the functioning of the immune system. This process allows lymphocytes to control all parts of the body in search of antigen, which allows them to develop and shape their response to the antigenic threat in specific conditions of the tissue microenvironment. After this, the lymphocytes can specifically return to the site with the microenvironment in which they found the antigen, which increases the selectivity of the immune response. Adhesive interactions between multiple receptors on circulating lymphocytes and their ligands expressed on the surface of endothelial cells in post-capillary venules are both a means of implementing the migration of leukocytes and a method of selectively controlling it. Despite the fact that significant progress has been made recently in the description of the cascade of processes necessary for the penetration of circulating leukocytes from the free bloodstream into the tissue, much in this process remains unclear. In particular, the currently known functions of adhesion molecules are not enough to explain all the recycling methods and binding specificity, the presence of which is shown both under normal conditions and under conditions of inflammation.

The initial temporary and limited interactions between the leukocyte in the bloodstream and the vascular wall are performed mainly by selectins and their glycoprotein ligands. At this stage, integrins and other molecules can take part. In the presence of corresponding signals, the next stage of strong adhesion can occur after the stages of rolling and testing, by binding the activated integrins to their ligands from the Ig superfamily. Locally elevated levels of immobilized cytokines and other chemoattractants may be involved in the initiation of such local activation, which is mediated by appropriate receptors and subsequent signal transduction within the cell. The final stage involves the process of migration of bound cells through the endothelial lining into the tissue through a very obscure mechanism. Tissue-specific circulation pathways are based on the regulation of the expression of certain receptor-ligand combinations of the adhesion molecule in the corresponding region of the body.

The interaction between the receptors on the surface of leukocytes and their ligands on the vascular endothelial cells is a crucial step in the mechanism for controlling the movement of lymphocytes between the blood and various lymphoid organs, and is also related to the transudation of leukocytes to the site of inflammation. A clone of a cDNA encoding vascular adhesion protein-1 (VAP-1), which mediates the adhesion reaction of a leukocyte-endothelial cell, is described. It turned out that the encoded VAP-1 has aminoxidase activity. In higher organisms, aminoxidase is believed to be involved in the metabolism of biogenic amines (McIntire W. and C. Hartmann, in Principles and Applications of Quinoproteins, VL Davidson, ed., Marcel Dekker, Inc., NY, Chap. 6 (1992)).

Adhesion tests performed with Ax cells expressing VAP-1 show that cDNA encodes a functional VAP-1 that can support interaction with its ligand on PBL and leads to stable binding of PBL to Ax cells. However, there is no complete inhibition of such increased adhesion with MAT against VAP-1 (1B2 and TK8-14), which suggests that the VAP-1 molecule in rat Ax cells does not function exactly as it does in the native environment. It is possible that the modification of carbohydrates in the proteins of Ax cells, the local membrane environment, or the conformation of VAP-1 differ sufficiently from those in human HEV, so that MAT can no longer block all interactions of VAP-1 with its ligand. It is also possible that a subpopulation of VAP-1 molecules can exist in transfectants in a form lacking one or the other of the epitopes recognized by MAT 1B2 and TK8-14. Finally, the authors focused on the possibility that prolonged superexpression of VAP-1 in stable transfectants alters the expression of other adhesion molecules (s) on the surface of Ax cells. The function of other putative adhesion molecules, of course, will not be inhibited by MAT against VAP-1. HEV binding tests have shown that VAP-1 functions independently of lymphocytic L-selectin and mediates CD8 + PBL binding much better than CD4 + PBL. Ax cells obtained by transfection of VAP-1 cDNA reproduce these properties, since analysis of immunomagnetically purified L-selectin negative cells, as well as CD8 + and CD4 + cells, showed that L-selectin is not necessary for effective binding to Ax transfectants and that a subset of CD8 + cells in PBL is characterized by several times better adherence to VAP-1 transfectants than CD4 + cells (data not shown).
