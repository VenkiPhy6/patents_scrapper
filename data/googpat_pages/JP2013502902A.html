mycocidal FIELD OF THE INVENTION This invention relates to novel biological control agents having a mycocidal effect, compositions related thereto, and uses thereof. In particular, this agent has a bactericidal effect against Fusarium fungi. The invention further relates to isolated fungal inoculums, genes, proteins and / or organisms, and also to uses, methods and compositions comprising them.


oxysporum100wiltGros MichelCavendish Background of the Invention Fusarium is a filamentous fungus that is widely distributed in plants and soil. Certain Fusarium species are phytopathogens. For example, Fusarium oxysporum causes Fusarium wilt disease in over 100 plant species. It occurs by colonizing plant xylem, resulting in blockage and degradation. When this happens, symptoms such as leaf wilt and yellowing appear in the plant, ultimately leading to plant death. This condition was a major cause of the decline and disappearance of Gros Michel banana varieties from markets around the world. Recently, new strains have begun to attack the dominant Cavendish variety of plants, and it is feared that if this is not resolved, this variety will also disappear from the global market.

root rotcircinatumpitch canker80   Fusarium root rot is a major cause of seedling mortality in forest seed nurseries and also causes a decline in survival during the first growing season after transplantation. This disease is caused by several Fusarium species and is common in many parts of the world. This disease is particularly problematic in Western Canada and the United States, and in the North Central and Southern States. In addition, pine pitch canker, caused by the fungus Fusarium circinatum, is a serious disease of pine trees and is a threat to the forest industry, particularly in the United States and New Zealand. Radiata or Monterey pine is highly susceptible to this disease and in some areas of California the death rate of mature trees reaches 80%.

It is. Corn and maize can be affected by a similar condition known as "ear rot". The above conditions can reduce crop yield and grade and can contaminate the grain with mycotoxins. The cereal industry is estimated to cost nearly $ 5 billion annually for FHB. In recent years, FHB has become a growing and growing problem for commercially important wheat and barley crops in Western Canada. In the past, Fusarium oxysporum and Fusarium avenaceum have been identified as two species mainly associated with FHB in Canada, where yields have fallen more than 30% in some affected fields. FHB and earlot harvested grains are often contaminated with mycotoxins such as deoxynivalenol (DON) trichothecenes associated with feed rejection, general digestive disorders, diarrhea, and bleeding. The emergence of the toxin-producing 3-acetyldeoxynivalenol (3-ADON) Fusarium graminearum population in North America is a fairly recent phenomenon. It is replacing the 15-ADON chemical type. In addition, the mycotoxin profile of F. culmorum, which is similar to F. graminearum, both in laboratory and field conditions, is a further threat to corn, maize and wheat production worldwide. . Currently there are no effective control measures for FHB and related mycotoxins. In addition, there are no corn, wheat, barley, oat, or other small grain resistant varieties. Fungicides can be effective, but can only temporarily control disease.

Fusarium is one of the most drug-resistant fungi, it is difficult to treat Fusarium infections, and invasive infections can be fatal.
