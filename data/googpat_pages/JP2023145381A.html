1 g/L In the field of chemical manufacturing, efforts are being made to switch from petroleum-derived raw materials to biomass-derived raw materials. Patent Document 1 discloses a technology for fermentatively producing phenol from glucose using a microorganism with a modified sugar metabolic pathway. The production amount of phenol using this technology is estimated to be around 1 g/L in batch production.

The main objective of the present disclosure is to provide a technology that can be used for the production of phenol from biomass.

[Phenol production method]
The present disclosure also provides a method of producing phenol by fermentation of biomass using the above-described high phenol producing microorganism.

Biomass refers to organic resources derived from plants and animals that can be recycled into energy and materials, excluding fossil resources. In this disclosure, biomass specifically refers to resources containing fermentable carbohydrates that can be subjected to microbial fermentation. Examples of fermentable carbohydrates include, but are not limited to, glucose, xylose, sucrose, starch, blackstrap molasses, glycerol, ribitol, and erythritol. The biomass may include lipids, amino acids, organic acids, and alcohols that may be derived from or generated by the metabolism of these fermentable carbohydrates. These biomass can be used alone or in combination of two or more.




Fermentation may be carried out by culturing microorganisms by a conventionally known method, and phenol is secreted and accumulated in the fermentation aqueous medium through the metabolism of biomass by the microorganisms.
The aqueous medium (medium) containing biomass used for culture is a solid medium or liquid medium containing sufficient nutrients, including at least one type of carbon source, in which microorganisms can grow.
The concentration of the biomass raw material in the medium is not particularly limited as long as it can produce phenol. The concentration is, for example, 0.05 to 20 (w/v)%, preferably 0.1 to 15 (w/v)%, more preferably 0.2 to 10 (w/v)%. The reason for using 0.2 (w/v)% or more is because the tyrosine production ability of microorganisms increases, and the reason for using 10 (w/v) or less is that even if it is added more than that, the effect will increase dramatically. This is because it is not recognized.
Furthermore, additional biomass may be added as the biomass decreases as the reaction progresses.

The fermentation process using microorganisms may be a process that involves the growth of live bacteria or a process that involves a dormant cell reaction using previously cultured bacteria. For the dormant cell reaction, a culture solution of a microorganism cultured in advance may be used as it is, or cells recovered by filtration or centrifugation may be used. Cultivation and resting cell reaction may be performed by conventionally known methods.



[Phenol]
The present disclosure also provides phenol obtained by the above-described method for producing phenol.
The produced phenol and its produced amount can be detected and measured using conventional methods such as high performance liquid chromatography and LC/MS. Phenol can be separated and purified by appropriately combining well-known operations such as filtration, centrifugation, vacuum concentration, ion exchange or adsorption chromatography, solvent extraction, distillation, and crystallization as necessary.
