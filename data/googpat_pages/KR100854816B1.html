Allergic mechanisms are usually classified into four types, type I to type IV. Type I allergy is involved in IgE antibodies and is represented by hay fever, asthma, measles, anaphylactic shock, and the like. Type II allergy involves IgG antibodies and IgM antibodies, which are cytotoxic reactions that activate the complement system, such as fetal erythrophilia or autoimmune hemolytic anemia. Type III allergies are tissue injuries caused by antigen-antibody complexes and are representative of alux reactions, serum diseases, and glomerulonephritis. Type IV allergy is a delayed allergy (delayed hypersensitivity) in which T cells are involved and is a response typified by tuberculin response or contact dermatitis. Among allergies, it is known that Type I, Type II and Type IV are involved in the development of food allergy. On the other hand, it is known that the type I development mechanism is mainly involved in allergies such as allergic to the environment, namely hay fever, atopic dermatitis, bronchial asthma, allergic rhinitis and allergic conjunctivitis.

IgE IgE . .The type I allergy is characterized by induction of allergen specific IgE and release of chemical mediators such as histamine or leukotrienes. The immune response is due to interactions by various cells, and one of the cells involved therein is a helper T cell (Th). Helper T cells are classified into T cell subpopulations and have the function of recognizing antigens to produce various cytokines (helper factors) to regulate the induction of immune responses. Helper T cells are classified into Th1 cells and Th2 cells according to cytokine production capacity. Th2 cytokines such as IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13 are required for the class switch of antibodies to occur in B cells to produce IgE antibodies. In fact, it is known that Th2 cells are increased in lymphocytes of allergic patients. That is, allergens invaded from the outside are presented to T cells in a state where some of them are bound to MHC class II molecules by antigen presenting cells such as dendritic cells and macrophages, and Th2 cells are activated and differentiated. Th2 cytokines released by Th2 cells induce the class switch of B cells to produce IgE antibodies, which bind to Fc R on mast cells in the tissues or on the surface of basophils. Allergens are recognized by IgE antibodies bound to the surface of mast cells or basophils upon subsequent invasion, forming crosslinks between the IgE antibodies. This stimulus triggers the explosive release of chemical mediators from mast cells or basophils, resulting in various allergic symptoms.
