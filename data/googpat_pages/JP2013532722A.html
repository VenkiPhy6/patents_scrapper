TuberculosisInternational Journal of PharmaceuticsJournal of Antimicrobial ChemotherapyTuberculosis   Tuberculosis affects the body's organs and several different symptoms appear, but the site of initial infection is the lungs. Tuberculosis that affects the lungs is known as pulmonary tuberculosis. Pulmonary tuberculosis is the most prevalent form of tuberculosis (Tuberculosis, 2005, 85, p. 227-234). Current chemotherapeutic regimens for treating pulmonary tuberculosis include co-administration of primary anti-tuberculosis drugs (isoniazid, rifampicin, esambutol and / or pyrazinamide) for 4 months, followed by isoniazid, rifampicin and / or esambutol 2 Depending on the type of tuberculosis, the treatment period can be further extended and can range from 9 months to 2 years. This chemotherapeutic regimen is administered once a day in an oral dosage form with a short plasma half-life ("International Journal of Pharmaceutics, 2004, 276, pp. 41-49). And side effects due to overdose (Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy, 2004, 54, p. 761-766). These side effects are due to undesirable biodistribution characteristics. Furthermore, for orally administered drugs, only a small amount of drug reaches the site of action (ie, lungs) and disappears within a few hours (Tuberculosis, 2005, 85, p. 227-234). It has been observed. The above problems are related to poor patient compliance and lead to the development of multi-drug resistant tuberculosis (MDRTB).

Multidrug resistant tuberculosis fact sheetTreatment of drug resistant tuberculosis, fact sheet   MDRTB is a form of tuberculosis that is resistant to at least two of the best anti-tuberculosis drugs (isoniazid and rifampicin) ("Multidrug resistant tuberculosis fact sheet", Center for Disease Control, 2008) . MDRTB is treated with fluoroquinolones, amikacin, aminoglycosides such as kanamycin and capreomycin, secondary anti-tuberculosis drugs such as p-aminosalicylic acid and thioacetazone ("Treatment of drug resistant" tuberculosis, fact sheet), Center for Disease Control, 2007). Secondary tuberculosis drugs are associated with dose-related side effects and poor bioavailability in the lungs and are an obstacle to eradication of the disease.

International Journal of Antimicrobial Agents   Similar to the problem of pulmonary tuberculosis, the problem of side effects caused by poor bioavailability of drugs and high doses also occurs in the treatment of MRSA and MSSA pneumonia. Nosocomial pneumonia due to MRSA and ventilator-associated pneumonia are associated with high mortality (International Journal of Antimicrobial Agents, 2007, 30, p. 19-24), which is insufficient Depends on the treatment.

US Respiratory Disease   Beta-lactams (such as ampicillin and cephalosporin) are very effective against MSSA pneumonia as first line therapy. Vancomycin is considered as the next therapy, but is not as effective as beta-lactam in infections caused by MSSA. Vancomycin is excreted in the urine by glomerular filtration and is not metabolized. In addition, vancomycin does not relatively penetrate into lung tissue ("US Respiratory Disease", 2006, p. 62-64). In summary, MRSA / MSSA pneumonia therapy has several drawbacks, such as poor pulmonary bioavailability of drugs, toxicity due to drug dosage, and the like.
