TECHNICAL FIELD The present invention relates to genetically modified forsythia and a method for producing useful secondary metabolites using the same.

Sesamum indicum Sesamin contained in sesame (Sesamum indicum) seeds has been shown to have various physiological activities, and is known to be effective in improving cholesterol metabolism, liver function, immune function, etc. . A method of separating and purifying sesamin from sesame seeds or strained lees of sesame seeds has already been put into practical use (see Patent Documents 1 and 2). are commercially available. However, what can be supplied as a raw material for sesamin is a by-product in the refining process of sesame oil products, and it is feared that supply will become difficult due to fluctuations in demand for sesame oil products. Moreover, sesame seeds are usually harvested only once a year. Moreover, Japan relies on imports for most of the sesame seeds it uses. Therefore, considering the increase in demand for sesamin in the future, securing a stable source of raw material supply will be an issue.

U18i-CPi-FkLEDLED So far, we have developed a forsythia gene-recombinant cell U18i-CPi-Fk as a sustainable source of sesamin (Patent Document 3, Non-Patent Document 1). The same producer showed high sesamin-producing ability under red LED and had the property that it could be stored for a long time under liquid nitrogen using sodium alginate beads. On the other hand, large-scale cultivation facilities are required for large-scale cultivation, and uniform red LED irradiation is impossible in large-scale cultivation, so there is a possibility that production efficiency will decrease significantly. There were drawbacks.

(CYP81Q1) In recent years, there have been a series of reports on the production of plant secondary metabolites by introducing a large number of target substances into microorganisms such as yeast using synthetic biology techniques, but no microorganisms that produce useful sesame-derived lignans have been developed. In addition, even if such microorganisms were developed, it would be difficult to cultivate them on a large scale for commercial use. There is a concern that the production efficiency of the target substance will be significantly reduced. So far, there is an example of producing sesamin by introducing a sesame-derived biosynthetic enzyme gene (CYP81Q1) into Arabidopsis thaliana, but it was a fragile producer because the amount was very small. In view of the above background, the development of transgenic plants that efficiently produce sesamin has been desired.

An object of the present invention is to provide a transgenic woody plant capable of producing non-endogenous useful secondary metabolites. The present invention includes the following inventions in order to solve the above problems.

[Genetically modified forsythia]
The present invention provides transgenic forsythia that produce non-endogenous useful secondary metabolites. The transgenic forsythia of the present invention may be one in which a foreign gene for producing a non-endogenous useful secondary metabolite is introduced into the genome so as to be expressible. The genetically modified forsythia of the present invention can be vegetatively propagated by cuttings or hydroponics, and the ability to produce useful secondary metabolites is maintained even in the next generation of vegetatively propagated genetically modified forsythia. Forsythia suspensaForsythia viridissimaForsythia koreanaForsythia japonicaForsythia togashiiForsythia europaeaForsythia koreanaForsythia intermedia Forsythia is a general term for perennial woody plants belonging to the genus Forsythia of the family Oleaceae, and is a deciduous broadleaf shrub that is hardy, has high fertility, and is easy to cultivate. Representative species include Forsythia suspensa, which is native to China, Forsythia viridisima, Forsythia koreana, which is native to the Korean Peninsula, Forsythia japonica, which is native to Japan, and Forsythia japonica. Forsythia togashii and Forsythia europaea, which is native to Europe, are known. In the home country, Forsythia koreana is commonly planted as the so-called "forsythia". As a horticultural species, Forsythia intermedia, which is a hybrid of Forsythia intermedia and Forsythia intermedia, is widely distributed.

Forsythia is a woody plant that expresses pinoresinol-lariciresinol reductase and pinoresinol glycosidase, accumulates a large amount of pinoresinol, and accumulates triterpenes, monoterpene glycosides, lignans, and the like as secondary metabolites. These components are used in traditional Chinese medicine as antipyretics, antiphlogistic agents, diuretics, drainage agents, and analgesics, and have long been used as crude drugs exhibiting strong antibacterial action.

Sesamin is a major lignan contained in sesame seeds and is known to have various physiological activities as described above. Piperitol is a sesamin precursor in the lignan metabolic pathway with sesamin as an intermediate (see FIG. 1). Sesamin and piperitol can be produced by introducing a sesamin synthase gene (eg, CYP81Q1 gene or CYP81Q2 gene) into forsythia.

Sesamolin, sesaminol, and sesaminol triglucoside are also lignans contained in sesame seeds, sesamolin is a precursor of sesaminol, and sesaminol triglucoside is a sesaminol glycoside. Sesamolin and sesaminol can be produced, for example, by introducing the CYP81Q1 gene and the CYP92B14 gene into forsythia (see FIG. 1). Sesaminol triglucoside can be produced, for example, by introducing the UGT71A9 gene, the UGT94AG1 gene and the UGT94AA2 gene into forsythia into which the CYP81Q1 gene and the CYP92B14 gene have been introduced.

Purbiatilol can be produced, for example, by introducing the CYP81Q3 gene into Forsythia. Hinokinin can be produced, for example, by introducing the PCBER gene into Forsythia. Pulviatrid can be produced, for example, by introducing the CYP719A23 gene into Forsythia. The method for producing the genetically modified forsythia of the present invention is not particularly limited, and can be carried out using known plant genetic recombination techniques, plant cell culture techniques and plant regeneration techniques. For example, a foreign gene expression vector for producing a desired non-endogenous useful secondary metabolite is prepared, introduced into forsythia cells to obtain transformed forsythia cells, and cultured to obtain forsythia plant bodies. and redifferentiation. However, it is not limited to this.

An expression vector is constructed so as to contain a promoter that enables expression in plant cells and a gene to be expressed in plant cells. Various known vectors can be used as the parent vector of the expression vector. For example, plasmids, phages, cosmids and the like can be used, and can be appropriately selected according to the plant cell to be introduced and the method of introduction. Specific examples include pBR322, pBR325, pUC19, pUC119, pBluescript, pBluescriptSK, and pBI-based vectors. In particular, when the method of introducing a vector into a plant cell is a method using Agrobacterium, it is preferable to use a pBI-based binary vector. Specific examples of pBI-based binary vectors include pBIG, pBIN19, pBI101, pBI121, and the like.

The promoter is not particularly limited as long as it is capable of expressing the introduced gene in plant cells, and known promoters can be suitably used. Examples include cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter (CaMV35S), various actin gene promoters, various ubiquitin gene promoters, nopaline synthase gene promoters, tobacco PR1a gene promoters, napin gene promoters, oleosin gene promoters, and the like. Among these, cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter, actin gene promoter or ubiquitin gene promoter can be preferably used. By using each of the above promoters, any gene can be strongly expressed when introduced into a plant cell.

Expression vectors may contain other DNA segments in addition to promoters and genes to be expressed in plant cells. Other DNA segments are not particularly limited, but may include, for example, terminators, selection markers, enhancers, base sequences for enhancing translation efficiency, and the like. Moreover, the expression vector may further have a T-DNA region. The T-DNA region can enhance the efficiency of gene transfer, particularly when Agrobacterium is used to transfer the expression vector into plant cells.

The transcription terminator is not particularly limited as long as it functions as a transcription termination site, and known ones can be preferably used. For example, the transcription termination region (Nos terminator) of the nopaline synthase gene, the transcription termination region of cauliflower mosaic virus 35S (CaMV35S terminator), and the like can be preferably used. Among them, Nos terminator is more preferable. Placing a transcription terminator at an appropriate position in an expression vector can prevent the synthesis of an unnecessarily long transcript after introduction into a plant cell.

As a selectable marker, for example, a drug resistance gene can be used. Specific examples include drug resistance genes for hygromycin, bleomycin, kanamycin, gentamicin, chloramphenicol, and the like. Since the expression vector contains a drug-resistant gene, a desired transformed plant cell can be easily obtained by selecting plant cells that grow in a medium containing a selective drug.

Nucleotide sequences for enhancing translation efficiency include, for example, omega sequences derived from tobacco mosaic virus. Placing this omega sequence in the untranslated region (5'UTR) of the promoter can increase the translation efficiency of the target gene.

[Method for producing piperitol and/or sesamin]
The present invention provides a method for producing piperitol and/or sesamin. The production method of the present invention may include the following steps (1) and (2).
(1) a step of cultivating genetically modified forsythia in which a gene encoding sesame-sesamin synthase has been introduced into the genome so as to be expressible;
(2) A step of purifying piperitol, sesamin, or a mixture of piperitol and sesamin from the genetically modified forsythia.

In step (1), genetically modified forsythia, in which a gene encoding sesame-sesamin synthase has been introduced into the genome in an expressible manner, is cultivated. The genetically modified forsythia can be produced and used using known plant genetic recombination techniques, plant cell culture techniques and plant regeneration techniques, as described in the previous section. Alternatively, genetically modified forsythia (vegetatively propagated individuals) obtained by vegetatively propagating cuttings or hydroponically from the prepared genetically modified forsythia may be used. Fi35S:CYP81Q1 or Fk35S:CYP81Q1, which are the above deposited strains, may be used as the genetically modified forsythia, or genetically modified forsythia propagated from cuttings thereof may be used.

For the cultivation method of step (1), a known forsythia cultivation method can be used, which can be easily obtained from gardening-related books and gardening-related websites. The cultivation period in step (1) is not particularly limited, and can be appropriately set according to the cultivation environment. For example, it may be cultivated until the height reaches about 50 cm. It usually takes about 4 to 5 months for the cuttings to reach a height of about 50 cm. Alternatively, for example, the plant may be cultivated until mature leaves can be harvested. It usually takes about 1 to 2 months until mature leaves can be harvested from cuttings. When piperitol, sesamin, or a mixture of piperitol and sesamin is purified from leaves in step (2), after harvesting the leaves, step (1) can be repeated until the leaves can be harvested again.

In step (2), piperitol, sesamin, or a mixture of piperitol and sesamin is purified from the genetically modified forsythia cultivated in step (1). First, a portion of the plant is recovered from the plant that has undergone the cultivation period of step (1). The part of the plant to be collected is not particularly limited, and may be any part such as leaves, branches, roots, and flowers. preferable. The harvested plant parts may be subjected to a treatment suitable for extraction. Suitable treatments for extraction include, for example, chopping, crushing, drying, freeze-grinding, freeze-drying, and the like.

MurataPLOS One 10 e0144519 (2016) The method for extracting and purifying sesamin and piperitol from the transgenic forsythia, in which the gene encoding sesame sesamin synthase has been introduced into the genome in an expressible manner, is not particularly limited. It can be used by appropriately selecting from the following purification methods. For example, the method performed by the present inventors in Example 2 below can be cited. Specifically, freeze-dried leaves are crushed, 50% methanol is added, and the mixture is heated to 60 C. for extraction. Centrifuge and freeze the supernatant to dryness, resuspend in 50% methanol and filter. This filtrate corresponds to a purified mixture of piperitol and sesamin. When recovering piperitol and sesamin as respective purified products, they can be separated using, for example, high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). Specifically, for example, the method described in Murata et al. (PLOS One 10 e0144519 (2016)) can be used.

By using the production method of the present invention, it is possible to produce piperitol in approximately the same volume as sesamin. Since only a very small amount of piperitol can be recovered from sesame, the production method of the present invention is useful as a method for efficiently producing piperitol. Moreover, by using the production method of the present invention, sesamin and piperitol can be extracted from the leaves of genetically modified forsythia and purified. In sesame, sesamin is extracted from seeds, but the seeds can be harvested only once a year. On the other hand, transgenic forsythia leaves can be harvested all year round, making them excellent plants for constructing plant factories. In addition, since genetically modified forsythia can be propagated vegetatively by cuttings or hydroponics, it can be easily scaled up, making it an excellent plant for plant factories. Furthermore, compared to the production of sesamin and piperitol using microorganisms or cultured plant cells, the maintenance of transgenic forsythia can be carried out at low cost, so a significant cost reduction is expected.
